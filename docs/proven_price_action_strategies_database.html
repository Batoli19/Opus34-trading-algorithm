If an instrument's 12-month return minus its most recent 1-month return is positive → long. If negative → short. Hold for 1 month. Rebalance monthly. Size by inverse volatility. The 1-month exclusion prevents short-term reversal from contaminating the signal.
signal = (close.shift(21) / close.shift(252)) - 1 # 12mo excl. 1mo
vol = close.pct_change().rolling(60).std() * (252**0.5)
position = signal.apply(lambda x: 1 if x > 0 else -1) / vol
returns = position.shift(1) * close.pct_change()

NR7: today's high-low range is smaller than all previous 6 days. Market is coiling. Next session: enter a buy-stop above today's high and a sell-stop below today's low at the open. Whichever fires first is your trade. The ORB variant adds a time filter — only take the first breakout within 30 minutes of the open. The edge: volatility clusters. Tight days precede volatile days with above-random frequency.
daily_range = high - low
nr7 = daily_range == daily_range.rolling(7).min()
buy_stop = high.shift(1) # enter above prior day high
sell_stop = low.shift(1) # enter below prior day low
signal = nr7.shift(1) # trade the day AFTER NR7

Source:Linda Bradford Raschke & Connors — "Street Smarts" (1995) · Bulkowski "Encyclopedia of Chart Patterns" quantified failure rates on 44,000+ chart patterns · market microstructure research on stop cluster locations (Harris 2002 "Trading and Exchanges")
The mechanism: retail stops cluster just beyond visible swing highs/lows. Market makers and institutions know where stops live. Price raids these levels, triggers the stops (providing liquidity), then reverses because the actual supply/demand imbalance is in the other direction. The tell: price breaks the level, closes back inside within 1-3 bars, with an urgent reversal candle. Entry: on the close of the reversal bar. Stop: just beyond the spike. Target: opposite side of the prior range.
swing_high = high.rolling(lookback).max().shift(1)
swing_low = low.rolling(lookback).min().shift(1)
# Failed breakout up: price spikes above, closes below
failed_bull = (high > swing_high) & (close < swing_high)
# Failed breakout down: price spikes below, closes above
failed_bear = (low < swing_low) & (close > swing_low)
# Entry: market order on close · SL beyond spike wick

Two distinct behaviours based on gap size. Gaps <0.5% of price: institutional participants treat these as noise, price reverts to the prior close by end of session ~72% of the time. Gaps >2% with volume >150% of 20-day average: represent real information/order flow, price continues in gap direction ~63% of the time. The edge is knowing which regime you're in before the open. Volume at open confirms which game is being played.
gap_pct = (open - prev_close) / prev_close * 100
vol_ratio = volume / volume.rolling(20).mean()
# Small gap → fade (sell the gap up, buy the gap down)
fade_signal = (abs(gap_pct) < 0.5)
# Large gap + volume → ride continuation
run_signal = (abs(gap_pct) > 2.0) & (vol_ratio > 1.5)
# Target: prior close (fade) or 1.5× gap size (run)

An inside bar is entirely contained within the prior bar's range. It signals a pause in volatility. The critical question: which direction is the prior trend? Bullish prior move + inside bar → buy the high breakout with 62% win rate. The same setup against the prior trend only succeeds 38% of the time. Most retail traders trade inside bars blind to direction. Adding the trend filter doubles the edge. Best on daily charts; works on H4.
inside = (high < high.shift(1)) & (low > low.shift(1))
prior_trend = close.shift(1) > close.shift(6) # 5-bar trend
# With-trend entry
buy_break = inside & prior_trend & (close > high.shift(1))
sell_break = inside & ~prior_trend & (close < low.shift(1))
sl_buy = low.shift(1) # stop below prior bar low
sl_sell = high.shift(1)

VWAP (volume-weighted average price) is the reference price every institutional order management system targets. When price is 2 standard deviations above VWAP in a non-trending session, institutions have been systematically selling above their target — mean reversion to VWAP is structurally likely. The non-trending filter is critical: on trending days VWAP acts as support/resistance, not a magnet. Filter using ADX <25 or measuring price distance from session open.

lower2 = vwap - 2 * std

long_sig = (close < lower2) & adx_filter

Source:Linda Bradford Raschke & Larry Connors — "Street Smarts: High Probability Short-Term Trading Strategies" · 1995 · Raschke is CTA with 30-year audited track record · tested on S&P 500 futures, bond futures, currencies
Three consecutive bars each closing higher than the previous (or lower for short). Entry: buy-stop above the high of bar 3 (or sell-stop below low). Stop: below the low of bar 3. The logic is momentum persistence — after three consecutive closes in one direction, the fourth bar statistically continues more often than it reverses. Works best when the three bars are relatively contained in size (not already extended). Degrades on bar 4 entries, not bar 5+.
# Three consecutive higher closes → buy breakout
c1 = close > close.shift(1)
c2 = close.shift(1) > close.shift(2)
c3 = close.shift(2) > close.shift(3)
setup = c1 & c2 & c3
entry = setup & (close > high.shift(1)) # bar 4 breaks bar3 high
sl = low.shift(1) # stop below bar 3 low

What the data says does NOT work — the graveyard
Disproven
Single candlestick patterns — hammer, doji, shooting star, engulfing alone
Bulkowski tested 103 candlestick patterns on 4.7M candles. The overwhelming majority have win rates of 50–54% — statistically indistinguishable from a coin flip. A hammer with no volume confirmation, no support level, no trend context is noise. The ones that DO work (Abandoned Baby, Three White Soldiers) work because of multi-bar structure — not the single candle shape.
Marginal at best
RSI overbought/oversold — classic 70/30 signal used alone
Brock, Lakonishok & LeBaron (1992, Journal of Finance) tested this extensively. Standard RSI crossings at 70/30 produce ~52% win rate with no meaningful risk-adjusted return. RSI only works when combined with a trend filter (the Connors approach) or when using the ultra-short RSI(2) period.
Disproven
Moving average crossovers — 50/200 golden cross, EMA ribbons
Sullivan, Timmermann & White (1999) tested 7,846 technical trading rules including MA crossovers on 100 years of Dow Jones data. After correcting for data mining bias, no MA crossover strategy showed statistically significant returns. They work in hindsight because you can always find a parameter set that fit the past data.
Unvalidated
ICT / SMC concepts — order blocks, fair value gaps, breaker blocks
Zero peer-reviewed studies. Zero audited track records from practitioners who teach it publicly. The patterns are defined loosely enough that they can be found everywhere in hindsight. This does not mean they don't work — it means there is no independent quantitative evidence that they do. Your own 16-pass backtest is more rigorous than anything published by ICT proponents.
